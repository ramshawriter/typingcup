The em dash (—) is the long dash writers use to set off a thought — like this. Tap the copy button above to grab one, or type it yourself below.

## Quick answer

On **Windows**, hold <kbd>Alt</kbd> and type <kbd>0151</kbd> on the number pad. On **Mac**, press <kbd>Option</kbd> + <kbd>Shift</kbd> + <kbd>-</kbd> (hyphen). On **phones**, long-press the hyphen key.

| Device / app | What to press |

## Em dash vs en dash vs hyphen

These three look similar but do different jobs:

- **Hyphen (-)** joins words: *well-known*.
- **En dash (–)** shows a range: *pages 10–20*. Type it with <kbd>Alt</kbd> + <kbd>0150</kbd> (Windows) or <kbd>Option</kbd> + <kbd>-</kbd> (Mac).
- **Em dash (—)** breaks a sentence: *She paused — then left.*

## On Windows and Mac

On **Windows**, hold <kbd>Alt</kbd>, type <kbd>0151</kbd> on the numeric keypad, and release. On **Mac**, <kbd>Option</kbd> + <kbd>Shift</kbd> + <kbd>-</kbd> produces the em dash anywhere.

## On phones

Long-press the hyphen key on either iPhone or Android. A row of dash options appears — slide to the longest one (—).

## In Word and Google Docs

**Word** auto-corrects two hyphens between words into an em dash as you keep typing. In **Google Docs**, use Insert → Special characters and search "em dash".

## FAQ

### Should I put spaces around an em dash?
It's a style choice. US publishers usually set it tight (no spaces); many UK and web styles add a space on each side. Pick one and stay consistent.
